Phase 1 Threshold Foundation · Weeks 1–3
Objective: Re-establish threshold tolerance after the time trial. Intervals sit just below race pace to accumulate quality stimulus without taxing the hamstrings. Long runs build your Z2 aerobic base and fat oxidation efficiency — the foundation everything else is built on.

Phase 1 · Foundation
Cruise Repeats + Easy Base
✓ Done
▾
Interval
Cruise Repeats
5 × 5 min @ threshold pace — 90 sec walk recovery between reps. Start conservative and dial in your effort through the first two reps before committing to the pace.
Z3 Target Zone 5:20–5:30 /km 155–168 bpm
📊 Analytical Breakdown
🔁
Why 5 × 5 min: 25 total minutes of threshold stimulus. Research shows this rep structure allows full recovery between bouts while maximising time-in-zone. You're accumulating quality, not just volume.
❤️
Zone target (Z3, 155–168 bpm): Your cruise pace of 6:44 already sits at the Z2/Z3 border. These reps push you 8–15 bpm higher to stimulate threshold adaptation without crossing into the Z4 lactate accumulation zone.
📈
Adaptation goal: Raise your lactate threshold pace — the speed at which lactate production exceeds clearance. Currently ~5:26 /km (your TT pace). Target is 5:10 /km by Week 7.
🦵
Hamstring note: The 90-sec walk recovery (not jog) is deliberate — it allows full eccentric load dissipation between reps and keeps hamstring stress low this early in the block.
Lock the effort by HR, not speed. If the treadmill is set to 11.2 km/h but your HR is at 175+, ease to 10.9. The HR zone is the session — the pace number is just a guide.
Long Run
Easy Aerobic Base
7 km steady, fully conversational. No HR spikes permitted. Treadmill 1.0% incline throughout.
Z2 Aerobic 6:40–7:00 /km 140–155 bpm
📊 Analytical Breakdown
🔥
Why pure Z2: Z2 training maximises mitochondrial density and fat oxidation — the metabolic engine that powers every long race. Your body learns to spare glycogen by burning fat at faster paces. This is the unglamorous work that separates good race engines from great ones.
♻️
Recovery function: After the time trial, muscle damage is still being repaired. Z2 running increases blood flow to facilitate this repair without adding new stress. Think of it as active maintenance, not training.
💬
Conversational test: You should be able to speak full sentences without gasping. If you can't, you're in Z3. Slow down — there's no ego in getting this right.
If 7 km feels too easy — good. That's correct. The aerobic base you build here is what allows you to sustain Z4 pace for 5 km in Week 10.

Interval
Threshold Ladder
3 min / 5 min / 7 min / 5 min / 3 min @ 5:15–5:25 /km. 90 sec jog recovery between each rep. The pyramid structure builds to the hardest rep then descends.
Z3 base Z4 peak 5:15–5:25 /km 160–172 bpm
📊 Analytical Breakdown
🪜
Why a ladder: Pyramiding the rep length (3-5-7-5-3) is a classic structure because fatigue accumulates naturally. The 7-min centre rep is your biggest threshold challenge — approached when reasonably fresh, followed by shorter reps that maintain pace as fatigue increases.
🔄
Jog vs walk recovery: Switching from walk (Week 1) to jog recovery is significant. Jogging keeps lactate clearance active and teaches your body to run while already moderately fatigued — more race-specific than standing rest.
📉
Pace tightens: 5:15–5:25 vs 5:20–5:30 last week. A 5 sec/km improvement reflects the threshold stimulus from Week 1 beginning to translate. This is intentional progressive overload.
Volume stays the same this week — only the longest rep increases. Save yourself for the 7-min rep. If you're fading badly on the back half of the pyramid, you went too hard in the first 3-min.
Long Run
Progressive Long Run
7.5 km. First 5 km easy Z2, final 2.5 km drop to 6:10–6:20 /km (low Z3).
Z2 → Z3 6:40 → 6:15 /km
📊 Analytical Breakdown
📈
Progressive structure: Starting easy and ending faster teaches your body to run at tempo pace with glycogen already partially depleted — the exact metabolic state you'll be in at KM 3.5 of your time trial.
🎯
The final 2.5 km: 6:10–6:20 /km at the end of a 7.5 km run is harder than the same pace on fresh legs. This deliberate fatigue-state exposure builds mental and physiological resilience for the closing kms of a race.
Finish feeling like you had 1 km left in the tank. If you finish the last 2.5 km and immediately feel you could have gone faster — that's exactly right. Save the gas for the interval session.

Phase 1 · Foundation
Sustained Threshold + Steady Long
✓ Done
▾
Interval
Sustained Threshold Block
2 × 12 min @ 5:15 /km with 2 min jog recovery. The longest continuous threshold effort of Phase 1.
Z3–Z4 5:10–5:20 /km 162–174 bpm
📊 Analytical Breakdown
⏱️
Why 12 minutes: 12 continuous minutes at threshold is the physiological sweet spot — long enough to drive meaningful lactate threshold adaptation, short enough to repeat twice with quality. Studies by Jack Daniels show 10–20 min continuous threshold reps produce the most efficient LT gains per session.
🧪
Lactate dynamics: At Z4 (170–174 bpm), you're working right at maximal lactate steady state (MLSS). Sustaining this for 12 min teaches your body to clear lactate at rates approaching production — the defining adaptation of a faster 5K runner.
⚠️
HR guardrail: If HR exceeds 175 before the 12-min bell, ease 5 sec/km. Above 175 you've crossed into Z5 — the session becomes a different stimulus entirely and the second rep is compromised.
The 2-min jog recovery between reps is not rest — it's active recovery. Keep moving at 7:00–7:30 /km. Stopping completely resets your thermoregulatory and cardiovascular state and makes rep 2 harder than it should be.
Long Run
Steady State Long
8 km @ comfortable Z2. Consistent effort, not split-chasing. Lock to HR.
Z2 Aerobic 6:35–6:50 /km 142–157 bpm
📊 Analytical Breakdown
🏔️
Building the aerobic ceiling: 8 km of Z2 work at this stage is meaningful base volume. Each km adds mitochondrial density to your slow-twitch fibres — the cells that will carry you through the first 3 km of your time trial with controlled HR.
📊
Your aerobic efficiency data: At 150–157 bpm you're currently running 6:44 /km. The goal of this phase is to push that to 6:20–6:30 /km at the same HR by Week 7 — a direct indicator of improving aerobic fitness.
Your long run is your aerobic investment. Every easy km here pays dividends in Weeks 7–10. Don't skip it for short-term speed gains — the data consistently shows that runners who skip easy volume peak early and fade in competition.
Phase 2 Speed Development · Weeks 4–7
Objective: Push race pace toward 5:10–5:15 /km. Introduce controlled Z4–Z5 speed work. Long runs extend to 9–10 km to raise the aerobic ceiling. This is where your engine advantage gets converted into tangible pace gains.

1K Repeats
5 × 1 km @ 5:05–5:10 /km. 90 sec standing rest between reps. Full recovery — don't rush the rest.
Z45:05–5:10 /km170–178 bpm
📊 Analytical Breakdown
🎯
Format shift: Moving from time-based (5 min) to distance-based (1 km) reps is intentional. It teaches pace awareness rather than effort awareness — crucial for race execution where you'll be monitoring splits, not HR.
❤️
Entering Z4: At 170–178 bpm you're above lactate threshold. Each rep creates a lactate spike — the rest clears it. This "lactate flushing" mechanism trains your clearance pathway to be more efficient over time, raising your effective threshold pace.
⚖️
Even-split test: All 5 reps should feel identical in effort. Rep 1 hard = Reps 4–5 impossible. Start at 5:10, not 5:05. The target is controlled uniformity — race pace is not a sprint.
Each km should feel the same effort level. If rep 4 is noticeably harder than rep 1, you went out too fast. Consistency across all reps is the diagnostic — it tells you whether the pace is sustainable on race day.
Long Run

Z26:40–7:00 /km
Protect the hamstrings after 1K repeats. Easy is correct here. Your perceived exertion should never exceed 4/10. This run is aerobic maintenance — treat it like a walk with a slight upgrade.

This is not a time trial. You're making contact with sub-5:00 pace in a controlled environment. If the 400s feel genuinely easy, resist the urge to go faster — save that gear for race day.

20 minutes continuous: This is the key mental and physical benchmark of Phase 2. You've previously done reps up to 12 min (Week 3). Treadmill lock strategy: Set the speed, then look away from the display. Focus on breathing cadence (3 steps in, 2 out) or a fixed point. Watching the time countdown triggers mental resistance that compounds fatigue perception.
The discomfort is the adaptation. Minutes 12–16 will be the hardest. Know in advance that this is the expected experience — not a sign that something is wrong. Your cardiovascular system is being upgraded in real time.

Interval
Race Pace Repeats

📊 Analytical Breakdown
🎯
The most important session of the block: 5:10 /km is exactly 25:50 pace for 5K. You are training at your target. The 6 × 1 km format accumulates 6 km of race-pace running with recovery — more race-pace volume than you'll actually race. This is the strongest confidence signal you can generate before Week 10.
📈
Progression from Week 4: 5 reps → 6 reps, 90 sec rest → 75 sec rest. Both changes are meaningful. Less rest means your lactate clearance system must work faster. An extra rep means more cumulative time at race pace. Both adaptations transfer directly to race performance.
🧪
Race pace familiarity: One of the most underrated aspects of training is making the race pace feel "normal." After 6 km at 5:10 /km in reps, your neuromuscular system will have logged those patterns. Race day execution becomes recall, not discovery.
5:10 /km = sub-26 5K pace. You're training at your goal today. This session is the closest you'll get to race conditions before Week 10. Treat it with that respect — not as something to survive, but as something to execute with precision.

📊
Cardiac output signal: If your HR at 6:40 /km has dropped compared to Week 1 (even slightly), that's direct evidence of improved stroke volume — your heart is pumping more blood per beat. This is the adaptation that drives sustainable race pace improvement.
10 km done easy confirms you're ready to race 5 km fast. If 10 km at Z2 feels manageable, 5 km at Z4–Z5 is sustainable. The aerobic ceiling is now set for race day. Don't push this one.

✓ Done
▾
Interval
Speed-Endurance Mix
3 × 2 km @ 5:12 /km with 2 min jog recovery. Longer reps, slightly eased pace. Form under fatigue is the focus.
Z45:10–5:15 /km168–176 bpm
📊 Analytical Breakdown
📏
2 km reps simulate race segments: A 5K can be thought of as 2 × 2 km plus a 1 km close. Each rep here maps directly to that structure. By the 1.5 km mark of each rep you're running in a fatigued state — exactly what KM 3 and 4 of the race will demand.
🏃
Form focus: As fatigue sets in during the 2 km reps, your running mechanics typically degrade — heel striking increases, arm crossing increases, cadence drops. The coaching cue: keep cadence at 170+ spm in the last 400m of each rep regardless of discomfort.
Hold form late in each rep — that's where the race is decided. Sloppy form costs you 10–15 sec in a 5K. Specifically: keep your heel from overstriding in the final 400m of each 2 km rep. Short, fast, forward.

Interval
Activation & Strides

Z2 jogBrief Z5 strides
📊 Analytical Breakdown
⚡
Neuromuscular activation: The 30-sec strides prime your fast-twitch motor units without fatiguing them. Research consistently shows that 24–48 hr pre-race strides improve race performance by 1.5–3% compared to complete rest, through improved neuromuscular recruitment on race day.
🎯
Signal check: After the strides, you should feel fast, light, and energised — not tired. Tired after strides = undertapered. Fast and loose = ready. This is your readiness diagnostic for race day.
You should feel fast and loose after, not tired. If you feel heavy or flat, rest one more day. The strides are diagnostic — they tell you how ready you are. Trust what your body reports.
Time Trial
5K Time Trial — Sub-26:30
Full race-day protocol. Pacing: Km 1 @ 5:25 · Km 2–4 @ 5:15 · Km 5 sub-5:00. Split math: 5:25 + 5:18 + 5:15 + 5:12 + 5:00 = 26:10.
Z4 km 1–3Z5 km 4–55:17 avg /km
📊 Race Day Analytical Notes
🎯
Why start at 5:25: In March you likely went out at 5:15–5:20 and hit GI cramps at KM 2.0. Starting 10–15 sec/km slower for the first km keeps you in Z4 instead of redlining into Z5 — and the time cost is only ~5–8 seconds total versus the 30–60 sec you lose cramping and recovering.
📈
Negative split strategy: Runners who negative split (second half faster) in 5Ks outperform even-pace runners by an average of 4% in time trials. Your 5:01 final km under duress in March is direct evidence of your closing ability when properly paced into it.
🧠
KM 3 is the decision point: At KM 3 in a 5K, most runners either pull back (under-raced) or die (went out too hard). The correct response: hold exactly what you've been holding. The work is already done — execution is all that remains.
Don't go out at 5:10. The cramps at KM 2.0 in March were partly from early anaerobic overdraft triggering GI distress. Patience in KM 1–1.5 is the entire race. Your engine is ready. Let it build correctly.
